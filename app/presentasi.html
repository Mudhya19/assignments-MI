Problem Statement & Context

THE OPPORTUNITY:

  • Electronic Health Records contain longitudinal patient data with enormous research potential
  • Unsupervised algorithms from signal processing adapted for clinical use

THE CHALLENGE:

  • Clinical data has irregular sampling patterns (not routine)
  • High data missingness (healthcare utilization varies by patient)
  • Question remains: Which clustering algorithms perform best?
CRITICAL GAP: No systematic evaluation of unsupervised clustering on realistic clinical datasets with ground truth

Research Objectives

Objective 1: Algorithm Evaluation

  • Evaluate 30 unsupervised clustering algorithms
  • Test on 6,912 simulated clinical datasets
  • Focus: Temporal matching + partitional/fuzzy clustering

Objective 2: Real-World Application

  • Apply best algorithm to real clinical cohort
  • 43,426 pediatric patients with obesity
  • Identify BMI trajectory patterns ‚Üí MetS risk

Why Simulation?

  • Known ground truth = accurate accuracy measurement
  • Controlled parameters = understand performance under conditions

Methodology: Dataset Preparation

Clinical Measurements (3):

  • BMI (Body Mass Index)
  • SBP (Systolic Blood Pressure)
  • Random Glucose

Simulated Datasets:

  • 6 real trajectories per measurement type
  • 16-year span with yearly measurements
  • Total: 6,912 datasets

Real Cohort (MetS):

  • N = 43,426 children (ages 2-18)
  • 55.7% male, 73.9% Caucasian
  • Mean follow-up: 8.47 years | MetS cases: 3.4%

Methodology: How 30 Algorithms Built

30 Unique Algorithms = 3 Components √ó Options

Component 1: Assignment Method (2)

  • Partitional: Hard assignment (1 cluster per point)
  • Fuzzy: Probabilistic assignment (weights across clusters)

Component 2: Distance Measure (8)

  • DTW, DTW-LB, LB-Improved, LB-Keogh (temporal matching)
  • Euclidean, Manhattan, Soft-DTW, SBD (alternative metrics)

Component 3: Centroid Computation (6)

  • PAM, DBA, Soft-DTW Centroids + other methods

2 √ó 8 √ó 6 = 30 Total Algorithms

Evaluation Metrics

Adjusted Rand Index (ARI)

Measures: Agreement between predicted clusters & true clusters

Range: -1 to +1

  • +1 = Perfect clustering
  • 0 = Random clustering
  • -1 = Completely opposite

Interpretation:

ARI > 0.70
Good
ARI 0.50-0.70
Moderate
ARI < 0.30
Poor

Statistical Testing:

  • Nemenyi Test (multiple comparisons)
  • FDR correction
  • Consensus >70% = stable clusters

Critical Finding: Magnitude vs Shape

Magnitude-Based

(Classes differ by peak values)

  • ‚úì All algorithms performed well (ARI ~0.70)
  • ‚úì Missingness MINIMAL impact
  • ‚úì Easy to detect high vs low BMI

Shape-Based

(Classes differ by trajectory trend)

  • ‚úó Lower accuracies (ARI 0.55-0.60)
  • ‚úó 10% missing ‚Üí 40% accuracy DROP!
  • ‚úó Hard to detect rising vs stable trends

‚ö†Ô∏è CLINICAL IMPLICATION: If your EHR has missing data, use magnitude-based features (peak values) rather than shape-based features (trends)

Clinical Implications & Insights

Key Takeaway: Longitudinal > Static Measurements

Not just WHAT the BMI is, but HOW IT CHANGES matters!

Clinical Workflow:

  • 1. Identify patient trajectory patterns in your EHR
  • 2. Risk-stratify based on cluster membership
  • 3. Allocate interventions based on risk cluster
  • 4. Monitor for cluster transitions
  • 5. Measure intervention outcomes per cluster

‚úì Enables Precision Medicine: Identify clinically meaningful subgroups WITHOUT predefined labels | Discover hidden phenotypes | Stratify interventions

Limitations & Conclusions

Key Limitations

  • Limited trajectory diversity (6 per type)
  • Simple imputation method (mean only)
  • Selection bias in real cohort
  • MetS definition ambiguous
